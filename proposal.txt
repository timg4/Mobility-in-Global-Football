Global Player Mobility in Football: A League-to-LeagueNetwork Analysis
Project Proposal
Abstract
This project analyzes global player mobility in professional football using transfer data from the
Transfermarkt website. Player moves are represented as a directed, weighted network on league
level, where edges capture transfer flows between source and destination competitions across
seasons. Using standard network analysis methods (centrality measures, community detection,
and tier-flow indicators), the project aims to describe league roles such as exporters, importers,
and bridging markets, and to identify mobility regions and lower- to top-division pathways.
The analysis follows a CRISP-DM workflow and evaluates the results through hypothesis
testing, robustness checks, and comparison to a simple randomized baseline network.
Motivation and Problem Statement
Professional football can be seen as a global labor market, where players move between clubs
and leagues across countries. Many commonly used terms such as “selling leagues”, “import
leagues”, or “stepping-stone pathways” are mostly qualitative and not easy to reproduce. A
league-to-league network gives a clear and data-driven view of mobility pipelines and intercon-
nections across seasons. The dataset from Transfermarkt contains structured information on
players, clubs, leagues and transfers (including transfer type and fees), so it should be possible
to analyze movements between countries and also between lower and top divisions.
Research Questions
• RQ1 – Mobility structure / concentration: How is player mobility between leagues
structured as a directed and weighted network, and how concentrated are incoming transfer
flows across leagues?
• RQ2 – Centrality and roles: Which leagues primarily act as senders or receivers of players,
and which leagues function as bridges between otherwise weakly connected parts of the
network?
• RQ3 – Communities / mobility regions: Are there clusters of leagues that form strongly
connected mobility ecosystems and how stable are these clusters across seasons?
• RQ4 – Lower-league pathways: How important are transfers between lower tiers and top
tiers, and how do these pathways differ from top-tier mobility?
Hypotheses
• H1 – Concentration: Incoming transfer flows are highly concentrated, with a small set of
leagues accounting for a large share of all incoming transfers.
• H2 – Bridges: Some mid-level leagues exhibit disproportionately high betweenness central-
ity and act as bridges between regions and/or tier systems.
• H3 – Communities: The network exhibits non-random community structure, with detected
communities showing higher modularity and/or greater stability than in a randomized
baseline network.
• H4 – Lower-league pathways: Transfers originating from lower tiers are more likely to be
domestic and upward (from lower to higher tier) within the same country, while cross-border
moves are relatively less common compared to top-tier mobility.
Methodology
The project follows CRISP-DM and standard data science practice.
• Business understanding: Define scope (years, competitions/leagues, and which transfer
types to include). This is mainly determined by the available data.
• Data understanding: Inspect coverage, missing values, and consistency of league/club
identifiers and seasons. This was already done on a small subset to write this proposal.
• Data preparation: Map each transfer to source league →destination league for a given
season. Create edge lists per season and an aggregated graph. Include loans as an attribute.
Store fee information as an attribute.
• Modeling / analysis: Build directed weighted graphs per season and overall. Compute (i)
import/export rankings (weighted in/out degree), (ii) betweenness for bridge leagues, and
(iii) community detection (Louvain/Leiden). Report domestic vs. international shares and
tier-flow indicators (lower→top division).
• Evaluation: (i) Test the hypotheses using the computed metrics and clear summaries, (ii)
robustness checks (with vs. without loans; season-wise vs. aggregated), and (iii) baseline
comparison using a simple null model (randomized graph with preserved degrees) to show
whether the observed structure is stronger than random.
Expected Results and KPIs / Success Criteria
• Reproducible pipeline: End-to-end notebook that builds the league mobility graphs per
season from raw data.
• Core outputs per season: (a) summary table with top importers/exporters and bridge
leagues, (b) community assignment, (c) domestic vs. international share.
• Visual outputs: Clear figures: league-to-league flow heatmap and community/network
visualizations.
• Null model result: Report at a quantitative comparison vs. the randomized baseline (e.g.
higher modularity or stronger flow concentration than the null).
